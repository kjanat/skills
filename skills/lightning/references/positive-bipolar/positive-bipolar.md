# Positive and Bipolar Lightning

## Current Parameters (Table 5.1, Berger et al. 1975)

| Parameter                      | Units | 95%      | 50%      | 5%       | N  |
| ------------------------------ | ----- | -------- | -------- | -------- | -- |
| Peak current (min 2 kA)        | kA    | 4.6      | 35       | 250      | 26 |
| Charge (total)                 | C     | 20       | 80       | 350      | 26 |
| Impulse charge                 | C     | 2.0      | 16       | 150      | 25 |
| Front duration (2 kA to peak)  | us    | 3.5      | 22       | 200      | 19 |
| Max dI/dt                      | kA/us | 0.20     | 2.4      | 32       | 21 |
| Stroke duration (to half-peak) | us    | 25       | 230      | 2000     | 16 |
| Action integral                | A^2s  | 2.5x10^4 | 6.5x10^5 | 1.5x10^7 | 26 |
| Flash duration                 | ms    | 14       | 85       | 500      | 24 |

Positive stroke peak currents can far exceed negative: directly measured up to
340 kA (Goto & Narita 1995, Japan). NLDN reports up to 580 kA (less reliable).

## Prevalence

- Global fraction: <10% of all CG flashes
- US annual average (NLDN): 3-10%
- **Seasonal variation (US)**:
  - Summer (August): ~3% minimum
  - Winter (January-February): up to 25%
  - NE US in February: up to 80% positive (Orville et al. 1987)
- Usually single-stroke flashes (rarely multiple strokes)

## Conditions Conducive to Positive CG

| Condition                  | Mechanism                                                              |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Tilted dipole              | Wind shear exposes lower positive; negative charge displaced laterally |
| Shallow warm season clouds | Reduced negative charge above positive center (Midwest, Great Plains)  |
| Dissipating storms         | Lower positive center more accessible in decaying convection           |
| Winter thunderstorms       | Low cloud tops; charge centers close to ground (Japan Sea coast)       |
| Stratiform precipitation   | Positive charge below melting level; widespread                        |
| Contamination (smoke/dust) | NLDN anomalies near massive fires; mechanism debated                   |

## Positive Leader Characteristics

- Continuously propagating (no stepping like negative leaders)
- Speed: comparable to negative stepped leaders
- Much less luminous than negative leaders
- Corona streamers: shorter, less branched than negative corona
- Laboratory gap: positive breakdown voltage is lower than negative for same gap

## Positive Return Stroke

- Typically single stroke per flash
- Longer continuing currents than negative flashes
- Charge transfer: 80 C median vs 5.2 C for negative first strokes
- Action integral: 6.5x10^5 vs 5.5x10^4 A^2s (12x higher median)
- Front duration: 22 us vs 5.5 us (4x slower rise)
- Can transfer hundreds of coulombs (max observed ~350 C at 5%)

## Bipolar Lightning

- Flashes that transport both positive and negative charge to ground
- Successive strokes of opposite polarity, or polarity reversal within stroke
- Rarity: a few percent of CG flashes at most
- Often involves channel branching to separate charge regions
- May occur when storm has complex charge structure

## Association with Severe Weather

- Large positive flashes often precede tornadoes (by 10-30 min)
- +CG rate increase sometimes precedes hail/severe events
- Not a reliable sole predictor; high false alarm rate
- Positive flashes dominate in MCC trailing stratiform regions

## Sprites and Positive Lightning

- Most sprites triggered by positive CG flashes
- Required: large charge moment change (>120 C*km in <6 ms)
- Long continuing currents provide sustained charge transfer
- See em-environment/upper-atmosphere.md for sprite details
