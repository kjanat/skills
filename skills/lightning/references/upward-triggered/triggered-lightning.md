# Rocket-Triggered Lightning

## Overview

First triggered discharge: 1960, small rockets trailing grounded wires from
research vessel off Florida coast (Newman 1965). First over land: 1973,
Saint-Privat d'Allier, France.

## Two Triggering Techniques

### Classical Triggering (Grounded Wire)

1. Rocket launched trailing thin conducting wire connected to ground
2. Rocket speed: ~150-200 m/s; typical wire length: 400-700 m
3. Ambient field threshold for success: ~5-10 kV/m at ground (natural
   conditions typically >=4 kV/m under active storms)
4. Upward positive leader from wire tip → initial continuous current (ICC)
5. Wire explodes ~1-2 ms after leader inception; arc channel replaces wire
6. After ICC (if any), downward dart leaders + return strokes follow
7. Subsequent strokes very similar to those in natural lightning

### Altitude Triggering (Ungrounded Wire)

1. Wire not connected to ground; short floating segment (~50-400 m)
2. Bidirectional leader: upward positive + downward negative from wire ends
3. Downward leader: stepped, like natural first-stroke leader
4. Produces first stroke more similar to natural first strokes
5. More difficult to achieve; requires higher ambient fields

Success depends on: ambient E-field, storm proximity, rocket trajectory,
wire length.

## Comparison: Triggered vs Natural

| Parameter           | Triggered (subsequent)  | Natural (subsequent)    |
| ------------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Peak current        | 12 kA (GM)              | 12 kA (median)          |
| Max dI/dt           | ~100 kA/us              | 40 kA/us (median)       |
| Charge              | ~1 C                    | 1.4 C                   |
| Return stroke speed | (1-2)x10^8 m/s          | (1-2)x10^8 m/s          |
| Channel base height | Ground                  | Ground                  |
| Initial stage       | ICC (no stepped leader) | Stepped leader + 1st RS |

Key difference: classical triggering does not produce the equivalent of a
natural first return stroke (which follows a stepped leader). Altitude
triggering can produce a stepped-leader-like first stroke.

## Scientific Applications

### Measurement Advantages

- Known strike point: instrument immediately around channel base
- Current measured directly at ground (shunt resistor)
- Simultaneous multi-parameter: current, EM fields, optical, acoustic
- Repeatable conditions for channel-base characterization

### Key Results from Triggered Lightning

- Validated NLDN calibration (peak current vs. field relationship)
- M-component mechanism confirmed (counter-propagating waves)
- Step voltage/touch voltage measurements for grounding studies
- Lightning interaction with power lines: direct injection experiments
- Soil ionization and transient grounding impedance measurements
