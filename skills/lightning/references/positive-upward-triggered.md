# Positive Lightning, Upward Lightning & Triggered Lightning

## Positive CG Lightning

### Fraction of CG Flashes

- Global average: <10%
- US annual average (NLDN): 3-10%
- Summer US (August): ~3% minimum
- Winter US (Jan-Feb): up to 25-80%
- NE US in February: up to 80% positive (Orville et al. 1987)
- Winter Japan (sea coast): up to 60%
- Usually single-stroke flashes (rarely multiple strokes)

### Conditions Conducive to Positive CG

| Condition                  | Mechanism                                                              |
| -------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Tilted dipole              | Wind shear exposes lower positive; negative charge displaced laterally |
| Dissipating storms         | Lower positive center more accessible in decaying convection           |
| Winter thunderstorms       | Low cloud tops; charge centers close to ground (Japan Sea coast)       |
| Shallow warm season clouds | Reduced negative charge above positive center (Midwest, Great Plains)  |
| Stratiform precipitation   | Positive charge below melting level; widespread                        |
| Severe storms              | Pos can dominate >30 min                                               |
| Contamination (smoke/dust) | 25-67% positive in affected regions; mechanism debated                 |

| Impulse charge                 | C     | 2.0      | 16           | 150          |
| Front duration (2 kA to peak)  | us    | 3.5      | 22           | 200          |
| Max dI/dt                      | kA/us | 0.20     | **2.4**      | 32           |
| Stroke duration (to half-peak) | us    | 25       | 230          | 2000         |

5% peak current 250 kA vs 80 kA for negative → factor >3.
Highest directly measured: ~300 kA (Japan: 340, 320, 280 kA; Goto & Narita 1995).
NLDN reports up to 580 kA (less reliable).

### Five Properties of Positive Lightning

1. **Single-stroke**: usually 1 stroke/flash (vs 3-5 for negative)
2. **Continuing current**: always present; can reach tens of kA
3. **Preceded by IC activity**: >100-200 ms before return stroke
4. **Long horizontal channels**: up to tens of km
5. **Leader mode**: can propagate continuously or stepped (unlike neg)

### Positive Leader Characteristics

- Continuously propagating (no stepping like negative leaders)
- Speed: comparable to negative stepped leaders
- Much less luminous than negative leaders
- Corona streamers: shorter, less branched than negative corona
- Laboratory gap: positive breakdown voltage lower than negative for same gap

### Positive Return Stroke

- Typically single stroke per flash
- Longer continuing currents than negative flashes
- Charge transfer: 80 C median vs 5.2 C for negative first strokes
- Action integral: 6.5x10^5 vs 5.5x10^4 A^2s (12x higher median)
- Front duration: 22 us vs 5.5 us (4x slower rise)
- Can transfer hundreds of coulombs (max observed ~350 C at 5%)

### Return-Stroke Speed

Positive: ~0.8x10^8 m/s. Negative: ~1.7x10^8 m/s (short segments).

### Association with Severe Weather

- Large positive flashes often precede tornadoes (by 10-30 min)
- +CG rate increase sometimes precedes hail/severe events
- Not a reliable sole predictor; high false alarm rate
- Positive flashes dominate in MCC trailing stratiform regions

### Sprites and Positive Lightning

- Most sprites triggered by positive CG flashes
- Required: large charge moment change (>120 C·km in <6 ms)
- Long continuing currents provide sustained charge transfer
- See `references/distant-em-upper-atmo.md` for sprite details

## Bipolar Lightning

- Flashes that transport both positive and negative charge to ground
- Successive strokes of opposite polarity, or polarity reversal within stroke
- Rarity: a few percent of CG flashes at most
- Often involves channel branching to separate charge regions
- May occur when storm has complex charge structure

### Upward Negative Lightning (Most Common Upward Type)

Sequence:

1. Upward positive leader from tower → initial continuous current (ICC)
2. ICC: typically hundreds of A, duration hundreds of ms
3. May be followed by one or more downward dart leader-return stroke sequences
4. These subsequent strokes similar to those in downward lightning

- Upward negative leader from tower tip
- Associated with overhead positive charge
- More common in winter (Japan): dominant type in winter storms

### Current Reflections in Tall Objects

- Lightning channel impedance: 600 ohm to 2.5 kohm
- Tower impedance: ~300 ohm
- Bottom: reflection coefficient = (Zch - Zgr)/(Zch + Zgr) ≈ -1 (short circuit)
- Top: reflection coefficient positive (impedance mismatch)
- At CN Tower (553 m): current pulses separated by ~3 us (2H/c)
- Current at tower top NOT significantly affected by tower height
- Current at bottom contaminated by reflections
- "Undisturbed" current: current that would flow at tower base if tower were
  replaced by equivalent lightning channel

### Tower Influence on Lightning Parameters

- Peak current at tower base ≈ (1+rho_top)/(1-rho_bot·rho_top) × undisturbed current
- For ground reflection -0.7 and top reflection +0.5: enhancement factor ~1.9
- Tall tower measurements may overestimate CG current peaks
- Berger's data (Monte San Salvatore) may have less enhancement due to
  lower effective reflection coefficients

### EM Fields from Strikes to Tall Objects

- Field waveforms differ from those of strikes to flat ground
- Near-field: additional early pulse from tower current reflections
- Far-field: initial polarity can reverse for tall towers
- Must use modified transmission line models accounting for tower

## Rocket-Triggered Lightning

### History

First triggered discharge: 1960, small rockets trailing grounded wires from
research vessel off Florida coast (Newman 1965). First over land: 1973,
Saint-Privat d'Allier, France.

### Classical Triggering

- Rocket at ~150-200 m/s trailing grounded wire (~0.2 mm diameter)
- Typical wire length: 400-700 m
- At 200-300 m: enhanced field → upward positive leader
- Wire vaporizes ~1-2 ms after leader inception; arc channel replaces wire
- After ICC (if any), downward dart leaders + return strokes follow
- Ground E-field threshold: **4-10 kV/m** (Florida)
- Triggering potential at rocket altitude: **-3.6 MV**

### Altitude Triggering

- Ungrounded wire + insulating gap → bidirectional leader
- Short floating segment (~50-400 m)
- Positive charge up, negative charge down
- Downward leader: stepped, like natural first-stroke leader
- Produces first-stroke-like events (vs classical → subsequent-stroke-like)
- More difficult to achieve; requires higher ambient fields

### Triggering Success Rate

### Triggered vs Natural Comparison

| Parameter           | Triggered (subsequent)  | Natural (subsequent)    |
| ------------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Peak current        | 12 kA (GM)              | 12 kA (median)          |
| Max dI/dt           | ~100 kA/us              | 40 kA/us (median)       |
| Charge              | ~1 C                    | 1.4 C                   |
| Return stroke speed | (1-2)x10^8 m/s          | (1-2)x10^8 m/s          |
| Channel base height | Ground                  | Ground                  |
| Initial stage       | ICC (no stepped leader) | Stepped leader + 1st RS |

Key difference: classical triggering does not produce equivalent of natural
first return stroke. Altitude triggering can produce stepped-leader-like
first stroke.

### Key Finding: Grounding Independence

GM peak currents: **10-14 kA** regardless of grounding resistance
(0.1 ohm to 64 kohm). Lightning lowers its own grounding impedance via
fulgurites, surface arcing (up to 20 m), underground plasma channels.

### Scientific Applications

Measurement advantages:

- Known strike point: instruments immediately around channel base
- Current measured directly at ground (shunt resistor)
- Simultaneous multi-parameter: current, EM fields, optical, acoustic
- Repeatable conditions for channel-base characterization

Key results:

- Validated NLDN calibration (peak current vs field relationship)
- M-component mechanism confirmed (counter-propagating waves)
- Step voltage/touch voltage measurements for grounding studies
- Lightning interaction with power lines: direct injection experiments
- Soil ionization and transient grounding impedance measurements

### Unsuccessful Techniques (as of 2003)

- Laser beams (plasma sigma ~10^-3 S/m vs copper 5.8x10^7)
- Microwave beams
- Water jets
- Transient flames
