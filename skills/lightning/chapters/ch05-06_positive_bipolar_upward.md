## Chapter 5: Positive and Bipolar Lightning Discharges to Ground

### 5.1 Introduction & Occurrence

- Positive flashes: transport positive charge from cloud to Earth
- **Less than 10% of global CG lightning is positive**
- First well-documented study: Berger et al. (1975) — 26 positive discharges on Monte San Salvatore, Switzerland
- Berger & Garbagnati (1984) ultimately assigned all 67 positive flashes at MSS to upward category
- Italian tower measurements: 5 upward positive + 7 downward positive discharges
- Gorin & Shkilev (1984): 9 of 90 flashes (10%) from Ostankino tower were positive
- Podgorski & Landt (1987): 1 of 15 CN tower flashes (1985) was positive
- No positive discharges in South Africa (1972-82), though 27% of 44 flashes had indeterminate polarity
- Peissenberg tower: 3 upward positive discharges (no leader-return-stroke sequences)
- Brazil (1985-94): 1 positive flash vs 22 negative

**Four reasons positive lightning matters:**

1. Highest directly measured currents (**~300 kA**) and largest charge transfers (**hundreds of coulombs or more**)
2. Dominant CG type during cold season, dissipating storms, etc.
3. Preferentially related to **sprites** (middle-atmosphere luminous phenomena)
4. Identification by locating systems (NLDN) has implications for meteorological studies

### 5.2 Conditions Conducive to Positive Lightning

Five situations with enhanced positive lightning:

1. **Dissipating stage** of thunderstorms — after main negative charge removed by negative CG
2. **Winter thunderstorms** — positive charge displaced by wind shear
   - Sea of Japan coast: max **60% positive** in December (Hojo et al. 1989)
   - France: max **44%** in February (Le Boulch & Plantier 1990)
   - NE United States: **~80% positive** in February (Orville et al. 1987)
   - Two central US winter events: 59% and 29% positive
   - About 1/3 of winter lightning currents at Japan towers (88 m, 200 m) were positive
3. **Shallow clouds** (trailing stratiform regions of MCSs)
4. **Severe storms** — positive dominant for >30 min in some supercells
5. **Forest fire / smoke contaminated clouds**
   - Yellowstone fires: **25%** positive (~50 flashes)
   - Prescribed fire, Ontario: **19 of 28 (68%) positive**

### 5.3 Characterization of Positive Lightning

**General properties (5 observed):**

1. **Usually single stroke** — 80% of negative flashes have >=2 strokes; positive almost always 1
   - Heidler et al. (1998): 32 of 36 positive = 1 stroke, 4 had 2 strokes (mean interstroke interval 102 ms)
   - Lyons et al. (1998b): 1002 (~0.04%) of 2.7M positive had >10 strokes (likely misidentified cloud)
2. **Followed by continuing current** — typically tens to hundreds of ms
   - Positive CC can exceed **10 kA** for up to 10 ms (Brook et al. 1982)
   - Single flash charge transfer >300 C in first 4 ms (winter Japan)
   - Charge transfers of order **1000 C** reported (Miyake et al. 1992, winter Japan)
3. **Preceded by significant in-cloud activity** — avg >100 ms (Fuquay) or >200 ms (Rust et al.)
4. **Long horizontal channels** — up to tens of km
5. **Leaders can move continuously or stepped** (vs. negative leaders always stepped in virgin air)

**Two categories of current waveforms (Berger):**

- **Microsecond-scale**: similar to negative, risetime ~10 us
- **Millisecond-scale**: risetimes up to hundreds of us — M-component mode of charge transfer
  - Associated with very long (1-2 km) upward negative connecting leaders

**Upward connecting leaders in positive lightning:** up to ~2 km (vs. 20-70 m for negative)

### 5.3.2 Comparison of Positive and Negative Leaders

From Berger & Vogelsanger (1969), Fig. 5.8:

| Leader type       | n  | Speed (m/s)       | Step length (m)   | Interstep interval (us) |
| ----------------- | -- | ----------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------- |
| Downward negative | 19 | 6x10^4 - 1.1x10^6 | —                 | —                       |
| Upward positive   | 7  | 4x10^4 - 1x10^6   | 4-40              | 40-120                  |
| Downward positive | 1  | 4x10^5 - 2.4x10^6 | no distinct steps | —                       |
| Upward negative   | —  | 8x10^4 - 4.5x10^5 | 3-20              | 30-50                   |

- Downward positive leader (only 1 observation): considerably faster, speed increased from **4x10^5 to 2.4x10^6 m/s** approaching ground
- Positive leaders are considerably less luminous than negative leaders
- 26-30% of positive field waveforms show leader step pulses prior to return stroke

**Leader step pulse intervals:**

- Positive: mean **26 us** (Cooray & Lundquist 1982)
- Negative: mean **14 us**

**3 MHz radiation duration:**

- Negative return strokes: 60-600 us, mean 137 us
- Positive return strokes: **5-25 ms** (much longer)

**Triggered lightning leader speeds:**

- Upward negative leader (initiating positive): ~10^5 m/s
- Upward positive leader (initiating negative): initially ~2x10^4 m/s

### 5.3.3 Positive Leader Mechanism (Long Sparks)

Structure of continuously moving positive leader (Gorin et al. 1976):

- **Leader tip (head)**: volume ~1 cm^3
- **Leader channel**: ~1 mm diameter, T ~ **10,000 K**, conductivity ~ **10^4 S/m**
- **Streamer zone**: bundle of streamers, T ~ **1000 K or less**, essentially insulating
- Leader channel radiates in **visible + near-IR**; streamer zone in **UV**
- Stepping in positive leader influenced by humidity (higher humidity -> more stepping)

### 5.3.5 Peak Current

**Directly measured positive peak current distributions unreliable for moderate-height objects** — Berger's 26 events are a mix of two very different discharge types.

**Triggered positive flashes (winter Japan, Matsumoto et al. 1996):**

- Three flashes: peak currents **32, 27, 54 kA**; durations >60 ms, no leader-RS sequences
- One flash: oscillatory waveform, initial positive peak **132 kA**, risetime ~20 us, CC ~10 kA for 35 ms
- Duration of oscillatory part: 110 us

**Triggered positive flash (summer Florida, Bejleri 1999):**

- Peak ~6 kA, overall risetime ~25 us, duration several tens of ms
- Initial rise to ~2 kA in ~1 us

### 5.4 Bipolar Lightning Discharges to Ground

- First reported: McEachron (1939, 1941) at Empire State Building
- 11 of 80 (14%) over 10 years were bipolar; no purely positive flashes observed
- Berger (1978): 72 of 1196 (6%) were bipolar, 68 upward type
- Gorin & Shkilev (1984): 6 of 90 (6.7%) upward from Ostankino were bipolar
- Peissenberg: 2 bipolar events
- Winter Japan: 5-33% bipolar (Table 8.1)
- At least 1 bipolar per triggered-lightning experiment in France, Japan, NM, FL, China

**Berger (1978) bipolar statistics (30 events, Table 6.6):**

- Median negative current peak: **350 A**
- Median positive current peak: **1.5 kA**
- Median negative charge: **12 C**
- Median positive charge: **25 C**
- All 30 had **initial negative** polarity

**Three types of bipolar discharge:**

1. **Polarity reversal in slowly varying current** (ms-scale) — ICC or continuing current
   - Example: positive+negative charge transfers of 235 and 240 C (Hubert et al. 1984)
   - Superimposed positive pulse peak 17 kA, risetime 40 us
2. **Different polarity initial stage vs return stroke** — e.g., negative ICC followed by positive RS (peak 27 kA)
3. **Return strokes of opposite polarity** — all documented cases are upward type
   - CN tower: strokes of -10.6, +6.5, -8.9 kA; interstroke intervals 300, 335 ms
   - Florida triggered: positive 3rd of 8 strokes, interstroke intervals 374 and 369 ms (vs 39-101 ms for negative)

**Matsumoto et al. (1996) oscillatory event:**

- Two cycles of damped oscillation, duration 110 us
- First cycle: positive peak **132 kA**, following negative peak ~45 kA
- Followed by positive CC ~10 kA for 35 ms

## Chapter 6: Upward Lightning Initiated by Ground-Based Objects

### 6.1 Introduction — Height Thresholds & Effective Height

- Objects 100-500 m: experience both downward and upward flashes
- **<100 m**: assumed only downward
- **>500 m**: assumed only upward
- Fraction of upward flashes increases with object height (Table 2.3)

- Critical distance for breakdown from object tip: **~15 m** for objects 50-300 m height (Bazelyan et al. 1978)

- South Africa tower: 14% upward, 32% downward, 54% indeterminate (1978-82)
- Italian towers: **~60%** upward (Berger & Garbagnati 1984), vs Eriksson's estimate of 98%
- Most upward discharges originated from man-made structures, not mountain peaks
- Upward lightning initiation triggered by **rapid E-field changes from in-cloud discharges**, not slower charge buildup

### 6.4 Impulsive Currents

Three categories of current pulses in upward lightning:

1. **ICC pulses (alpha-components)**: superimposed on initial-stage current
2. **Return-stroke pulses (beta-components)**: preceded by zero-current interval
3. **M-component pulses**: superimposed on continuing current after RS

**Peissenberg tower statistics (Fuchs et al. 1998):**

### 6.5 Lightning Current Reflections in Tall Objects

**Transmission-line behavior:**

- Tower behaves as distributed circuit when height >= shortest wavelengths (initial rise)
- Different waveforms at different heights on Ostankino (47, 272, 533 m)
- Median peaks: **18 kA at 47 m** vs **9 kA at 533 m** (factor 2 difference)

**Lightning channel equivalent impedance:**

- Gorin & Shkilev (1984): **600 ohm to 2.5 kohm** (assuming Z_tower = 300 ohm, R_gr = 0)
- Z_ch approximately **3 x Z_0** (tower characteristic impedance) — consistent across Ostankino and Peissenberg

**Current reflection coefficients:**

| Tower                              | Height (m) | rho_bottom           | rho_top                    |
| ---------------------------------- | ---------- | -------------------- | -------------------------- |
| CN Tower (Janischewskyj 1996, n=5) | 553        | 0.34-0.43 (avg 0.40) | -0.27 to -0.49 (avg -0.37) |
| Peissenberg (Beierl 1992)          | 160        | ~1                   | ~-0.5                      |
| Peissenberg (Fuchs 1998b, n=13)    | 160        | 0.64-0.81 (avg 0.70) | -0.39 to -0.68 (avg -0.53) |

- CN tower: Z_ch/Z_0 ratio = 1.7-2.9
- Current wave propagation speed in Peissenberg tower: **278 m/us** (nearly speed of light)
- Round-trip time for 160m: **1.15 us**

**Luminosity propagation at CN tower (Wang et al. 1995):**

- 45 events, within 180 m of top
- 7 exhibited dart-leader-RS sequences
- 38 exhibited slow M-component-like waveforms
  - Risetimes: 9-102 us, geometric mean **35 us**
  - 6 propagated upward; 32 bidirectional (origin tens of m above top)
  - Upward speed: 8x10^6 - 2.9x10^8 m/s, geometric mean **2.8x10^7 m/s**
  - Downward speed: 10^7 - 2.5x10^8 m/s, geometric mean **4.9x10^7 m/s**
  - Ratio up/down: 0.17-1.5, geometric mean **0.60**

**Berger's measurements unaffected by tower:**

- Melander (1984) modeling: 70m towers on MSS essentially unaffected
- 40m Italian towers: also unaffected
- 60m South Africa tower (bottom measurement): overestimated by factor **~1.6**
- Triggered-lightning geometric mean peaks: 9.9-15 kA across 6 experiments (structure heights 4.5-20 m) — no systematic height dependence

### 6.6 Electromagnetic Fields Due to Strikes to Tall Objects

- Austrian LLS: median peak current **12 kA** within 1 km of towers vs **9.8 kA** at 1-10 km (Diendorfer & Schulz 1998)
- More strokes per flash in tower strikes than ground strikes
- CN tower contribution to E-field at 2 km: **almost 2x** greater than channel contribution alone
- Enhancement due to: (i) two simultaneous wavefronts, (ii) near-doubling at bottom reflection, (iii) speed of light propagation in tower

### 6.7 Acoustic Output

- Some upward flashes produce sounds unlike usual thunder
- McEachron (1939): thunder produced by return strokes; no thunder if only ICC
- Davis & Standring (1947): discharges with currents <1 kA heard and seen; currents ~100 A not detected by human observers

### 6.8 Key Summary Points

- Objects >200 m on flat terrain or moderate height on mountaintops: primarily upward lightning
- Upward lightning always has initial stage; may or may not include RS sequences
- Positive RS speed: **~10^8 m/s**
- Bipolar discharges usually upward-initiated; positive and negative cloud charges tapped by different branches
- Current peaks at tower top are **not significantly affected** by tower presence (heights 4.5-540 m)
- Bottom measurements **are affected** — can overestimate by factor ~1.6
- Tall metallic objects **enhance** radiated EM fields relative to ground strikes
