Reference: Rakov, V.A. & Uman, M.A. (2003). *Lightning: Physics and Effects*. Cambridge University Press.

## Chapter 1: Introduction

### 1.1 Historical Overview

- Lightning present on Earth before life evolved (~3 billion years ago)
- Oldest known fulgurite: 250 million years old (Harland & Hacker 1966)
- Campanile of St. Mark, Venice (~100 m high): struck/damaged in 1388, 1417, 1489, 1548, 1565, 1653, 1745, 1761, 1762; Franklin rod installed 1766 — no damage since
- 1718: 24 church towers on Brittany coast damaged by lightning in one storm
- 1769: Church of St. Nazaire, Brescia — ~100 tons gunpowder exploded, ~1/6 of city destroyed, >3000 killed
- 62 m monument (London, 1677): never damaged by lightning
- Over 33 years: lightning struck 386 church towers, killed 103 bell-ringers (Fischer, 1784)
- 1799–1815: 150 cases of lightning damage to British naval vessels; 1 ship in 8 set on fire; ~100 lower masts destroyed; ~70 sailors killed; >130 wounded
- 1798: 44-gun ship *Resistance* exploded by lightning
- First systematic study: 10 May 1752, Marly-la-Ville, near Paris
- 6 August 1753: Georg Richmann killed by direct strike in St. Petersburg
- First photographic spectrum of lightning: Slipher (1917)
- First oscillographic lightning current recordings: tethered balloons in Russia (Stekolnikov & Valeev 1937) and England (Davis & Standring 1947), Empire State Building (McEachron 1939, 1941)
- First estimates of lightning peak current via residual magnetization: Pockels (1900)

### 1.2 Types of Lightning Discharge and Terminology

**Four types of cloud-to-ground lightning:**

| Type                  | Direction       | Polarity lowered | Estimated fraction         |
| --------------------- | --------------- | ---------------- | -------------------------- |
| (a) Downward negative | Downward leader | Negative         | ~90% or more of CG         |
| (b) Upward negative   | Upward leader   | Negative         | From tall objects (>100 m) |
| (c) Downward positive | Downward leader | Positive         | ≤10% of CG                 |
| (d) Upward positive   | Upward leader   | Positive         | From tall objects (>100 m) |

- ~75% of all lightning does not involve ground (cloud discharges / ICs)
- Three modes of charge transfer in negative subsequent strokes:
  - **(a) Dart-leader–return-stroke sequence**
  - **(b) Continuing current**: quasi-stationary arc, tens to hundreds of amperes, duration up to hundreds of ms
  - **(c) M-components**: perturbations/surges in continuing current; spatial front length ~1 km (vs ~10 m for dart leaders, ~100 m for return strokes)
- **Leader** conductivity: ~10⁴ S m⁻¹ (comparable to carbon)
- **Streamer**: much lower conductivity; air behind tip remains insulator
- Channel conductivity for active processes: ~10⁴ S m⁻¹
- Channel section ahead of dart-leader tip: ~0.02 S m⁻¹

#### Radio-Frequency Spectrum

- Peak at **5–10 kHz** (observed beyond ~50 km)
- Spectral amplitude ∝ **1/f** from peak to ~10 MHz
- Spectral amplitude ∝ **1/√f** from ~10 MHz to ~10 GHz

### 1.4 The Global Electric Circuit

#### 1.4.1 Atmospheric Conductivity

- Fair-weather E-field near surface: **~100 V/m** (downward)
- Conductivity at sea level: **~10⁻¹⁴ S/m**
- Conductivity at 35 km: **>10⁻¹¹ S/m** (>1000× sea level)
- Average Earth conductivity: **~10⁻³ S/m**
- At 100 km altitude: conductivity **12 ± 2 orders of magnitude** greater than at surface (comparable to Earth's surface conductivity)
- Electrosphere: highly conducting layer above **~60 km**
- Small ions: diameter 0.1–1 nm, lifetime ~100 s
- Free electrons attach to neutrals on timescale of ~µs below 50 km
- Ion production at sea level: **1 to 10 million pairs per m³ per second**
- Ocean ion production: **~½** of land production
- At 20 km: conductivity varies <±15% above storms (U-2 measurements)
- Storm perturbation of conductivity at 26–32 km: up to factor of 2

#### 1.4.2 Fair-Weather Electric Field

**Volland (1984) equation (Eq. 1.1):**

```
E(z) = −[93.8 exp(−4.527z) + 44.4 exp(−0.375z) + 11.8 exp(−0.121z)]
```

- E(z) in V/m, z in km; valid at mid-latitudes below ~60 km
- E at ground: **150 V/m**
- E at 10 km: **~3%** of ground value
- E at 30 km: **~300 mV/m** (mid-latitudes)
- E at 85 km: **~1 µV/m**

#### 1.4.3 Classical View

- Electrosphere potential: **~300 kV** (positive w.r.t. Earth)
- Most voltage drop below 20 km
- Earth's surface charge: **~5 × 10⁵ C** (negative)
- Fair-weather leakage current: **~1 kA** total (2 pA/m²)
- Charge neutralization timescale: **~10 minutes**
- Average thunderstorms active simultaneously: **~2000** (over ~10% of Earth's surface)
- Current from cloud tops to electrosphere: **~0.5 A** per thunderstorm
- Load resistance: 300 kV / 1 kA = **300 Ω**
- Carnegie curve: max ~1900 UT, min ~0400 UT
- CG lightning contribution to fair-weather E-field: **40 ± 10%** (hourly)
- Contribution to departures from mean: **25 ± 10%**

#### 1.4.4 Maxwell Current Density

**Eq. 1.2:**

```
J_M = J_E + J_C + J_L + ε₀ ∂E/∂t
```

- Under active storms: **~10 nA/m²**
- Under small Florida storms (no precipitation): **~1 nA/m²** or less
- At 16–20 km: J_E typically >½ of J_M
- At ground during active storms: displacement current dominates (below corona threshold of few kV/m)

#### 1.4.5 Modeling of Global Circuit

- Heights modeled up to 100–150 km
- Cloud top voltages: −23 to +79 MV (Marshall & Stolzenburg 2001)
  - Average: **+25 MV** (all 13 soundings)
  - Average for 9 positive cases: **+41 MV**
  - Average for stratiform clouds: **+32 MV**
- Kundt & Thuma (1999) estimated cloud top voltage ~1 MV (contested)
- ~½ current reaching ionosphere flows to conjugate hemisphere

### 1.5 Utilization of Lightning Energy

- Energy per CG flash: **10⁹–10¹⁰ J**
- Energy to run 5×100W bulbs for 1 month: **1.3 × 10⁹ J** (~360 kWh)
- Action integral (typical): **10⁵ A²s** (Table 4.4)
- Energy delivered to strike point: **10⁶–10⁷ J** (10⁻²–10⁻⁴ of total)
- 1 m² in Florida struck on average once per **10⁵ years**
- 60 m tower in Florida: struck about **0.5–1 times/year**
- Would need 12–24 such towers covering ~1 km² for 5 × 100W bulbs

## Chapter 2: Incidence of Lightning

### 2.1 Introduction

- Lightning is the most significant weather hazard to space vehicle launches (KSC experience)

### 2.2 Characterization of Individual Storms

#### Multicell storms

- Individual cell lifetime: ~1 hour
- Convective cells: ~km in diameter
- Updrafts: ≥10 m/s
- Thunderstorm speed over flat terrain: 20–30 km/hr
- Large frontal systems: persist >48 hours, move >2000 km

#### Flash rates

- Typical CG flash rates per storm: **1–10 flashes/min** for active storms
- Peak: up to **20+ CG flashes/min** for severe storms
- Total flash rates (including IC): can exceed **100 flashes/min** for intense storms
- Typical: **3–4 times** the CG rate (roughly)

#### Flash counting criteria

- **Electric field change detection threshold** determines flash counts
- Flash distinguished from stroke by time criterion: typically **>500 ms** separation → new flash
- Optical transient duration typically **200–300 ms** per flash

### 2.3 Thunderstorm Days (Td)

- **Isokeraunic level**: number of thunderstorm days per year at a location
- Maximum global: **>200 Td/yr** at Kampala, Uganda (242 Td/yr often cited)
- Central Florida: **80–100 Td/yr**
- Central Amazon: **>100 Td/yr**
- South Africa: **60–80 Td/yr**
- US average: **~40 Td/yr**
- Northern Europe: **5–25 Td/yr**
- Arctic/Antarctic: **0–2 Td/yr**
- Equatorial regions: typically **50–150 Td/yr**
- A "thunderstorm day" = a day on which thunder is heard at a station

### 2.4 Thunderstorm Hours (Th)

- Provides better temporal resolution than Td
- Typical ratio **Th/Td**: 1.1 to 1.4

### 2.5 Lightning Flash Density

#### Ground flash density (Ng)

- **Definition**: number of CG flashes per km² per year
- Most useful measure of lightning incidence for engineering applications

**Empirical relations:**

- **Ng = 0.04 Td^1.25** (Anderson et al. 1984) — most widely used
- **Ng = 0.023 Td^1.3** (Kuleshov et al. 2009 variant)
- For Florida (80 Td/yr): Ng ≈ **10–15 flashes/km²/yr**
- Typical temperate regions: **1–5 flashes/km²/yr**
- Global average over land: **~6 flashes/km²/yr**
- Maximum (central Africa, tropical): **>30 flashes/km²/yr**
- Over oceans: typically **0.1–1 flash/km²/yr** (factor 10 lower than land)

#### Total flash density (Nt)

- Nt = Nc + Ng (cloud + ground)
- Typically Nt ≈ 3–4 × Ng

### 2.6 Long-Term Variations

- Annual variations in flash rates can be **±30%** or more
- No definitive long-term trend established in available data
- Possible correlation with temperature: **~10% increase in flash rate per °C** warming (Price & Rind 1994)

### 2.7 Ratio of Cloud to Ground Flashes (z = Nc/Ng)

- **z typically ranges from 1 to over 10**, depending on location and storm type
- Commonly cited average: **z ≈ 3** (i.e., ~25% CG, ~75% IC)
- In tropics: z can be higher (5–10)
- In winter storms (Japan): z can be <1
- Relationship with storm depth proposed: deeper storms → higher z
- **Pierce (1970)**: z = 4.16 + 2.16 cos(3λ), where λ = latitude (useful only for rough estimates)

### 2.8 Characteristics as Function of Season, Location, Storm Type

- **Diurnal variation**: CG lightning peaks in **late afternoon** (14:00–18:00 local time) over land
  - Over oceans: peak in **early morning hours** (01:00–06:00 local time)
- **Seasonal variation**: peaks in **summer** (June–August in NH)
- **Positive CG fraction**: increases in **winter** and in **dissipating stages** of storms
  - Typical: **5–10%** positive CG in summer
  - Winter storms: can be **>50%** positive

### 2.9 Lightning Incidence to Various Objects

#### 2.9.1 Flat ground

- Probability of strike to 1 m² per year depends on Ng

#### 2.9.2 Tall structures

- **Empirical formula (Eriksson 1987)**:
  ```
  N = Ng × 24 × (h/10)^2.05 × 10⁻⁶
  ```
  where h = structure height in meters, N = strikes/year, Ng in flashes/km²/yr

- **Simplified**: N ≈ Ng × A_e × 10⁻⁶
  - where A_e = equivalent collection area

- **Example incidence rates**:

| Height (m) | Ng = 1 | Ng = 10 |
| ---------- | ------ | ------- |
| 20         | 0.01   | 0.1     |
| 60         | 0.1    | 1       |
| 100        | 0.3    | 3       |
| 200        | 1.2    | 12      |
| 500        | 8      | 80      |

- Structures >100 m also initiate upward lightning
- Empire State Building (443 m, NYC): struck **~23 times/year** (most are upward-initiated)
- CN Tower (553 m, Toronto): struck **~75 times/year**
- Ostankino Tower (540 m, Moscow): **~30 times/year**
- Monte San Salvatore towers (70 m, on 640 m mountain): **~100 flashes over 20+ years** (Berger)

#### 2.9.3 Power lines

- Typical: **10–20 flashes/100 km/year** for Ng = 5

#### 2.9.4 Aircraft

- ~1 strike per **1000–3000 flight hours** (discussed in Ch 10)

## Chapter 3: Electrical Structure of Lightning-Producing Clouds

### 3.1 Introduction — Cumulonimbus Characteristics

- Vertical extent of convective cloud systems: **3–20 km**
- Horizontal dimensions: **~3 km to >50 km**
- Frontal storm lines: can extend **hundreds of km**
- Cell lifetime: **~1 hour**
- Updraft velocity: **≥10 m/s** (definition of convective cell)
- Storm movement over flat terrain: **20–30 km/hr**
- Large frontal systems: persist >48 hours, move >2000 km
- Tropopause height: **~18 km** (tropics, summer) to **~8 km** (high latitudes, winter)
- Cloud tops can reach **up to 20 km** with vigorous updrafts
- Moist-adiabatic lapse rate: **~0.6 °C per 100 m**
- Supercooled water exists from **0 °C to −40 °C**
- All water frozen below **−40 °C**
- Most electrification occurs in **mixed-phase region** (0 to −40 °C)

### 3.2 Cumulonimbus

#### 3.2.1 Idealized Gross Charge Distribution

**Vertical tripole model** (Fig. 3.2a):

- **Main positive charge**: near cloud top (~12 km AGL in example)
- **Main negative charge**: middle (~7 km AGL in example)
- **Lower positive charge**: near cloud base (~2 km AGL in example)
- Typical charge magnitudes: main charges ~**40 C** each; lower positive ~**3 C**
- Lower positive charge may not always be present

**Reversal distance** for positive dipole (Eq. 3.4):

```
D₀ = (H_P × H_N)^(2/3) × (H_P^(2/3) + H_N^(2/3))^(1/2)
```

**Electric field at ground due to negative charge and image** (Eq. 3.2):

```
|E| = |Q|H / [2πε₀(H² + r²)^(3/2)]
```

#### 3.2.2 Inferences from Remote Measurements

- E-field beneath active thundercloud: **1–10 kV/m** on flat terrain (upward-directed)
- Fair-weather field: **~100 V/m** (downward)
- Typical field change at 20 km from lightning: **~100 V/m**
- Negative charge lowered to ground per flash: **−10 to −40 C** from 6–9.5 km ASL (−10 to −34 °C)
- Negative charge center altitude varies little flash-to-flash within a day, but varies day-to-day

**Charge locations from multi-station measurements:**

- Negative charge centers: layer **1–3 km thick**, lower boundary at **0 to −10 °C**
- Specific New Mexico data (Krehbiel et al. 1979): charges at **4.5–6 km AGL** (one at 3.6 km), temperatures **−9 to −17 °C**
- Florida (Jacobson & Krider 1976): charge from **6–9.5 km ASL**

#### 3.2.3 Inferences from In-Situ (Balloon) Measurements

**Simpson & Scrase (1937), Simpson & Robinson (1941) — England:**

- Main negative charge: **−1 to −20 °C** temperature range
- Main positive charge: above the negative charge

**Marshall & Stolzenburg (multiple papers, 1990s):**

- Found **4 to 10 charge layers** in convective regions (not just 3)
- Updraft regions: typically **4 charge regions** (alternating polarity)
- Outside updraft: up to **6–10 charge regions**
- Charge density: **1–3 nC/m³** typical for main charges
- Maximum charge density measured: **~10 nC/m³**
- Charge regions: **0.3–2 km** thick

**Key in-situ findings:**

- Vertical electric field inside cloud can reach **>100 kV/m** (see 3.2.4)
- Temperature of main negative charge center: consistently **−10 to −25 °C** across different geographic locations

#### 3.2.4 Maximum Measured In-Cloud Electric Fields

| Study                     | Location   | Max E-field    | Notes                        |
| ------------------------- | ---------- | -------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Winn et al. (1974)        | New Mexico | 100 kV/m       | Balloon; may not be true max |
| Marshall et al. (1995)    | Various    | 100–186 kV/m   | Several balloon soundings    |
| Stolzenburg et al. (2007) | Various    | up to 200 kV/m | Close to breakdown threshold |

- Conventional breakdown threshold at sea level: **~3 MV/m** (for uniform fields)
- At cloud heights (~6 km): conventional breakdown **~1 MV/m** (adjusted for air density)
- **Measured fields are 10–20% of conventional breakdown** → suggests local field enhancement or runaway electron processes

#### 3.2.5 Cloud Charges and Charge Densities

- Total charge in main negative region: **−20 to −300 C** (estimated)
- Berger (1977): average charge neutralized per CG stroke **~5 C** (but cloud has much more)
- Main positive charge typically equal to or greater than main negative
- Volume charge density in main negative region: **0.5–5 nC/m³** (typical)
- Volume of main negative charge region: **~10–100 km³**
- Screening layer charge at cloud boundary: **~1–10 C** total

#### 3.2.6 Electrification Mechanisms

**Ice-ice collision (non-inductive / graupel-ice) mechanism:**

- Most widely accepted mechanism for thundercloud electrification
- Requires: **mixed-phase region** (supercooled water + ice crystals + graupel/rimed ice)
- Charge separation per collision: **~1–100 fC** (10⁻¹⁵ C)
- Reversal temperature: **~−10 to −20 °C** — above this temperature (warmer), graupel charges negatively; below, positively
- Liquid water content threshold for charge sign reversal: depends on temperature
- Collision rates needed: **~10⁴ per m³ per second** to produce observed charge buildup in ~20 minutes
- Time to produce ~40 C charge: **10–30 minutes**

**Inductive mechanism:**

- Requires pre-existing E-field to polarize particles
- Contributes to but likely does not initiate electrification
- Charge per collision can be higher than non-inductive (~100 fC to pC range)

**Convective mechanism (Vonnegut):**

- Corona ions from ground carried up by updrafts
- Not generally accepted as primary mechanism

#### 3.2.7 Lower Positive Charge Center

- Located at **0 to −10 °C** (roughly 2–4 km AGL in summer storms)
- Several proposed origins:
  - Graupel charging below reversal temperature
  - Corona charge from ground reaching cloud base
  - Lightning depositing positive charge in lower cloud
- May play crucial role in initiating CG lightning (particularly for −CG)

### 3.3 Non-Cumulonimbus Clouds

#### Stratiform clouds

- Can be electrically active (Marshall & Stolzenburg 2001)
- Typical vertical field: **1–10 kV/m** (much less than Cb)
- Cloud top voltages: **+32 MV** average for electrified stratiform clouds
- Flash rates: typically **<0.1 per minute** (when lightning occurs)

#### Other sources of lightning-like discharges

- Volcanic eruptions
- Sandstorms
- Nuclear explosions
- See Chapter 20
