## Chapter 4: Downward Negative Lightning Discharges to Ground

### 4.1 Introduction

- Downward negative lightning: ~**90%** of all CG discharges
- Initiated in cloud, propagates downward, transports negative charge to ground

### 4.2 General Picture

**Flash structure:**

- Typical number of strokes per flash: **3–5** (observed range: **1–26**)
- Mean strokes/flash: **4.6** (Florida, Rakov & Uman 1990); **6.4** (New Mexico, Kitagawa et al. 1962)
- Single-stroke flash percentage: **17%** (Florida), **14%** (New Mexico), **21%** (Sri Lanka), **18%** (Sweden)
- ~**80%** or more of negative CG flashes contain >1 stroke
- ~**50%** of flashes create >1 termination on ground (separation up to many km)
- ~**33%** of multiple-stroke flashes have at least one subsequent stroke larger than first
- ~**20%** contain return strokes separated by tens to hundreds of µs
- First stroke typically **2–3×** larger than subsequent strokes (peak current and field)

**Flash duration and charge:**

- Total flash duration: typically **200–300 ms** (range: 10 ms to 2 s)
- Total flash charge: typically **20 C** (range: 1–400 C)
- Interstroke interval: typically several **tens of ms** (can be <1 ms to hundreds of ms)

**Energy:**

- Electrostatic energy per flash: **1–10 GJ** (for Q = 20 C, V = 50–500 MV)
- Marshall & Stolzenburg (2001): available energy range **10 MJ to 10 GJ**
- ~**70%** of return-stroke energy optically radiated (Paxton et al. 1986 gas dynamic model)
- Radio-frequency power at field peak: **2 × 10¹⁰ W** (first strokes), **3 × 10⁹ W** (subsequent)
- Peak optical power at field peak: ~2 orders of magnitude less than RF power
- Peak optical power: mean **2.3 × 10⁹ W** (first strokes), **4.8 × 10⁸ W** (subsequent, dart leaders)
- Satellite median optical power: **10⁹ W**; "superbolts" 10¹¹–10¹³ W range
- Channel pressure: **≥10 atm** initially
- Channel peak temperature: near or above **30 000 K**

### 4.3 Initial Breakdown

- Duration: **2–10 ms** (Clarence & Malan 1957); up to **400 ms** including intermediate stage
- May bridge main negative and lower positive charge regions
- Charge transfer during breakdown events: **2.0 C** over 1.5 km, **3.6 C** over 3.8 km (Krehbiel et al. 1979, two NM flashes)

**Initial breakdown pulses:**

- Electric field pulse duration: **20–40 µs** (Table 1.2)
- Interval between pulses: **70–130 µs**
- Largest pulses in a flash, occurring ms to tens of ms before first RS

### 4.4 Stepped Leader

#### 4.4.1 Speed

- Average downward speed: **2 × 10⁵ m/s** (range ~10⁵ to ~10⁶ m/s)
- May increase speed near ground (some Florida observations)
- Two-dimensional speeds from Proctor et al. (1988) in South Africa: mean **1.3 × 10⁵ m/s** for 60 leaders (range 8 × 10⁴ to 5 × 10⁵)

#### 4.4.3 Electrical Characteristics

- **Total charge**: median impulse charge **4.5 C** (Berger et al. 1975); 95%: 1.1 C; 5%: 20 C
- Brook et al. (1962): 24 first strokes, 3–20 C, GM = 6 C
- Proctor et al. (1988): 15 leaders, 3.6–57 C, median 11 C
- Channel length: 3–13 km, median 6 km (Proctor)
- **Average charge density**: ~**0.7 mC/m** (from 5 C / 7 km); Proctor: ~1 mC/m
- Thomson et al. (1985): near-ground line charge density 0.7–32 mC/m, GM = 3.4 mC/m
- **Average current**: ~**100–200 A** (143 A for 5C/35ms)
- Near-ground current: 100 A to 5 kA, mean 1.3 kA (Thomson et al. 1985; Krehbiel 1981)
- First strokes in multi-stroke flashes: ~7.2 C vs single-stroke: ~4.6 C (Brook et al.)

#### 4.4.5 Step Parameters

- Step length: typically **tens of meters** (~50 m)
- Step duration: ~**1 µs**
- Time between steps: **20–50 µs** (Table 4.3 below)
- Step current: ≥**1 kA** (inferred)
- Step charge: ≥**1 mC**

### 4.5 Attachment Process

- Upward connecting leader initiates from ground/objects in response to descending stepped leader
- Attachment height: ~**some tens of meters** above ground (more above tall structures)
- Electric potential difference between leader tip and ground: **~50 MV** (tens of MV)
- Range of cloud-to-ground potential: **50–500 MV** (Marshall & Stolzenburg: −102 to +94 MV max)

**Striking distance:**

- Various empirical formulas relate striking distance to return-stroke peak current
- Common form: **r_s = a × I_p^b** (where I_p = peak current in kA)

**Key observations from Table 4.4:**

- Median first-stroke peak current **2–3×** that of subsequent strokes
- First strokes transfer ~4× more total charge
- Subsequent strokes have **3–4×** higher max dI/dt
- Only a few % of negative first strokes exceed **100 kA**; ~20% of positive exceed 100 kA
- Directly measured currents: no peaks exceeding ~**300 kA**
- NLDN-reported maximum: 957 kA (negative), 580 kA (positive) — but estimated, not directly measured
- Berger's oscillograms limited to **0.5 µs** time resolution → underestimates dI/dt

**Supplementary current measurements:**

- Anderson & Eriksson (1980): median 10–90% risetime for subsequent strokes: **0.6 µs**
- Triggered lightning median 10–90% risetime: **0.3–0.6 µs**
- Triggered lightning median 10–90% dI/dt: **34–44 kA/µs** (Fisher; Leteinturier)
- Largest max dI/dt measured: **411 kA/µs** (Leteinturier, triggered, salt-water ground)
- Corresponding current: **>60 kA** (largest reported for summer triggered)
- South Africa 60-m tower: 50% peak current for 11 first strokes = **41 kA** (~30% higher than Switzerland; ~60% overestimate due to reflections)

**Dart-stepped leaders:**

- Step length: **5–10 m** (up to 25 m)
- Interstep interval: **2–8 µs**
- Step charge: few mC; step current: few kA
- Transition from dart to stepped mode sometimes in lowest ~400 m

**Chaotic leaders:**

- GM field peak at 100 km: **4.3 V/m** (relatively large subsequent strokes)
- GM previous interstroke interval: **23 ms** (for second strokes)
- Speeds: **3.2–5.0 × 10⁷ m/s** (among highest dart-leader speeds)

**Dart-leader narrowband radiation:**

- Begins average **265 µs** before return stroke (Le Vine & Krider 1977)
- Takagi: **100–1000 µs** prior to RS
- Above ~100 MHz: ceases ~100 µs before RS
- At 3 MHz: often continues during RS

### 4.8 Continuing Current

- Magnitude: typically **tens to hundreds of A**
- Duration: typically tens to hundreds of ms
- "Long" continuing current: arbitrarily defined as **>40 ms** (Kitagawa et al. 1962)
- **30–50%** of negative CG flashes contain long continuing currents
- Continuing current minimum threshold for M-components: ~**30 A** (with one exception at ~20 A)
- Maximum return-stroke stage duration: ~**3 ms**
- Corona sheath collapse time: ~**1 ms**
- Long CC initiated overwhelmingly by subsequent strokes (not first or single-stroke)
- Strokes initiating CC tend to have **smaller** field peaks
- Strokes preceding those initiating CC tend to have **larger** field peaks
- CC preceded by **shorter** interstroke intervals

**Waveshape categories (Fisher et al. 1993, triggered):**

- Type I: exponential decay with superimposed M-pulses
- Type II: hump with M-pulses + smooth decay
- Type III: slow increase/decrease with M-pulses throughout
- Type IV: hump with M-pulses + steady plateau
- Types I & II most common (25/30 cases)
- CC starts with M-component-like pulse, avg **1.4 ms** after RS peak

- M-components outnumber return strokes by **4:1**
- ~**1/3** of M-components transfer more charge than minimum subsequent RS impulse charge
- First M virtually always occurs ≤**4 ms** after RS
- M-field change duration: GM **0.9 ms** (Florida)
- M-field interval: GM **2.1 ms**
- **84%** of first M-components occurred within 3 ms of RS
- M-component current pulses: more or less **symmetrical**
- M-component luminosity nearly **invariant with height** below cloud base

**Mechanism:**

- Requires conducting channel to ground (carrying continuing current)
- Guided wave with spatial front length ~**1 km**
- Involves superposition of downward (from cloud) and upward (reflected from ground) waves
- In-cloud sources: fast negative leaders/streamers at **10⁶–10⁷ m/s** hitting channel top
- Or positive leaders extending from channel top, followed by negative recoil at **>10⁷ m/s**

### 4.10 J- and K-Processes

- **J-process**: redistribution of cloud charge on timescale of **tens of ms** after RS
- Often viewed as slow positive leader extending from flash origin into negative charge region
- **K-process**: faster transient; "recoil streamer" from positive leader tip back toward flash origin
- K-change duration: **0.5–2 ms**
- K-change interval: **2–20 ms** (GM ~6–8 ms in Florida)
- K-changes sometimes precede dart leader formation
- K-process speed: **2 × 10⁶ to 10⁷ m/s** (from VHF imaging)
- K-changes and M-changes produce similar E-field signatures at far distances

### 4.11 Regular Pulse Bursts

- Electric field pulse duration: **1–2 µs**
- Interpulse interval: **5–7 µs**
- Typically **20–40 pulses** per burst
- Occur later in flash
- Pulse amplitude relatively uniform within a burst
- May be associated with intermittent leader extension in previously formed channels

### Key Equations

**Transmission-line model (TL) relation between current and far field:**

```
E_rad(r,t) = −(µ₀ v)/(2π r) × i(0, t − r/c)    (Eq. 4.9 variant)
```

**Far-field to peak-current conversion:**

```
I_peak ≈ 2π r E_peak / (µ₀ v)
```

Commonly used: **I_peak (kA) ≈ 6.0 × E_peak** (at 100 km, V/m) — for v = 1.5 × 10⁸ m/s
